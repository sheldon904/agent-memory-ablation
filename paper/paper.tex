\begin{abstract}
\noindent A companion study characterized a five-channel hybrid memory substrate running
in production~[2] and closed by naming its single most important untested claim: that the
hybrid actually beats the baselines it subsumes, a comparison that report explicitly
declined to run against itself (``no comparison against an external baseline \dots\ has
been run,'' [2]~\S12; ``an external-baseline experiment (plain RAG over the same
corpus),'' [2]~\S13). This study runs exactly that experiment, and turns it on its author.
We build a frozen synthetic corpus of \textbf{1{,}583 operational facts} about a fictional
field-services company, with a fully known ground-truth relation graph (414 entities,
1{,}498 edges, a closed vocabulary of 22 relations over 11 entity types), and put
\textbf{three memory architectures behind one typed \texttt{MemoryProvider} interface}:
(a)~\textbf{stateless RAG} (chunked text, MiniLM embeddings in \texttt{sqlite-vec}, top-$k$
only); (b)~\textbf{graph-only} (the canonical-graph, closed-vocabulary posture,
forward-traversal recall, no vectors); and (c)~a \textbf{hybrid} that fuses facts $+$
vectors $+$ graph with weighted Reciprocal Rank Fusion. All three run against three seeded,
frozen query sets built before any arm was executed: \textbf{150 known-item},
\textbf{30 multi-hop} (2-3 joins), and \textbf{20 distractor} queries (answer absent; the
fabrication-pressure probe). Every recall is traced to an OpenTelemetry-shaped span
exported into the repository. The results do not flatter the hybrid. On this clean,
canonical corpus the \textbf{graph-only arm is the surprise strong performer}: it ties the
field on known-item recall (Recall@5 98.7\%, Recall@20 100\%), wins multi-hop retrieval
outright (53.3\% terminal-fact success vs.\ the hybrid's 40.0\% and RAG's 0.0\%), and, most
consequentially, is the \textbf{only arm that does not fabricate}, refusing correctly on
100\% of distractors against just 10\% for both RAG and the hybrid. The hybrid's entire
measured advantage reduces to ranking precision (best MRR 0.800, best Recall@1 71.3\%), for
which it pays the most latency ($\approx$9\,ms p50, ${\sim}30\times$ the graph arm's
$\approx$0.3\,ms) and the most storage (2{,}528 bytes/fact, $25\times$ the graph arm's
101), and inherits RAG's fabrication pressure wholesale. We state four hypotheses up front,
report that two are refuted, inventory every failure, devote a section to \emph{where the
hybrid loses}, and disclose the corpus properties that make this the graph's best case.
Every number reproduces from \texttt{make eval} on the checked-in seeds.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
The Sheldon Dynamics memory program has, to date, produced two \emph{characterization}
studies, a fourteen-run evaluation of an operational decisioning substrate against a frozen
holdout~[1], and a seventeen-day in-vivo instrumentation of a production hybrid memory
substrate~[2]. Both study a \emph{single system}: they measure what a shipped architecture
does, in careful detail, but neither pits that architecture against the alternatives it
claims to improve on. [2] is explicit and repeated about this being its central limitation.
Its threats-to-validity section lists, eighth and last, ``no comparison against an external
baseline (hosted memory API or plain RAG over raw text) has been run,'' and its
recommended-next-steps section ends with ``(5) an external-baseline experiment (plain RAG
over the same corpus) using the E1 harness unchanged.''

This report is that experiment, deliberately generalized from ``plain RAG vs.\ the hybrid''
to a three-way ablation, and deliberately pointed back at the author's own design
assumptions. The motivating question is narrow and falsifiable: \emph{when you strip a
production hybrid memory down to its constituent postures, pure vectors, pure canonical
graph, and run all three head-to-head on one frozen corpus with one frozen eval harness,
does the hybrid actually earn its complexity, and where does it not?}

The scientific value of the study is precisely that it can embarrass its author, and partly
does. A hybrid that swept every metric would be a red flag: it would suggest a benchmark
tuned to the conclusion. What follows instead is an ablation in which the simplest structured
arm wins most axes on this corpus, the hybrid's advantage is real but narrow and expensive,
and the corpus itself is disclosed as the graph's most favorable case. That combination, a
genuine cost paid, honestly reported, is the point. Synthetic is a feature, not a
concession: unlike a production store, a synthetic corpus with a generated ground-truth
graph is shareable, re-runnable, and gives every query a machine-checkable answer, including
the distractors whose correct answer is ``not in memory.''

\section{Hypotheses}
Stated before results, judged against them in \S11.
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{H1: Vectors win known-item.} The vector-bearing arms (RAG, hybrid) will
  lead known-item retrieval (Recall@5, MRR); the closed-vocabulary graph, lacking dense
  semantics, will trail on lexically-paraphrased single-fact queries.
  \item \textbf{H2: Structure wins multi-hop.} Graph and hybrid will beat RAG on
  multi-hop retrieval by a wide margin, because joins require traversal that top-$k$ vector
  recall cannot perform.
  \item \textbf{H3: Refusal is ordered graph $>$ hybrid $>$ RAG.} Graph-only will refuse
  most correctly on distractors (absence from a canonical graph is an unambiguous signal),
  RAG will fabricate most, and the hybrid will land \emph{between} the two.
  \item \textbf{H4: The hybrid does not dominate.} It will not win every metric; it will
  pay measurable costs in latency, storage-per-fact, and at least one quality axis relative
  to the specialized arms.
\end{itemize}

\section{The three arms}
All three implement one typed interface, \texttt{MemoryProvider} (\texttt{arms/base.py}):
\texttt{build(facts)} indexes the frozen corpus once; \texttt{recall(query, k)} returns a
ranked list of facts plus a single boolean, \texttt{refused}, the arm's own decision that it
has no confident answer. The harness derives every metric from that response and the
wall-clock time of the call, so no arm sees ground truth or can special-case the evaluation.

\paragraph{(a) Stateless RAG (\arm{rag}).} Each corpus fact is one retrieval chunk (the
facts are sentence-sized). Facts are embedded with \texttt{all-MiniLM-L6-v2} (384-d, CPU,
L2-normalized) and stored in a \texttt{sqlite-vec} (\texttt{vec0}) index; recall is
exact-cosine top-$k$. There is no graph, no entity linking, no state between queries. A
single cosine floor (0.40, matching the source system's semantic threshold~[2]) is both the
relevance cutoff and the entire refusal rule: if the best match is below the floor, the arm
reports ``not in memory.'' That one threshold is RAG's only defense against fabrication.

\paragraph{(b) Graph-only (\arm{graph}).} The corpus's ground-truth relation graph is loaded
into a forward (subject$\rightarrow$object) adjacency structure. A query is linked to seed
entities by a shared, purely-lexical linker (id patterns like \texttt{WO-10047}, plus whole
display-name matches on word boundaries); recall is a bounded forward breadth-first traversal
that collects each reached node's \emph{outgoing} facts, ranked by hop distance then
token-Jaccard overlap. No vectors. Forward-only traversal is deliberate: every relation
points outward from an entity to its resources (a job to its equipment, a site to its owner),
so it follows every reasoning chain while avoiding the reverse fan-in explosion through hub
nodes, the same hub-suppression instinct [2] encodes, expressed here as edge directionality.
The arm refuses exactly when the query links no entity.

\paragraph{(c) Hybrid (\arm{hybrid}).} A ported slice of the five-channel design~[2], facts
$+$ vectors $+$ graph, with three grounded channels: a BM25 lexical index over fact text, MiniLM cosine (shared embedder with RAG), and the graph traversal (shared engine with the
graph arm). The three channel orderings are combined by \textbf{weighted Reciprocal Rank
Fusion}~[3], with the graph channel up-weighted 2:1 because it is the precision-oriented
channel and the only one that can reach a multi-hop answer. This is the parameter-free fusion
[2]~\S13 named as its own top recommended improvement. The refusal rule is deliberately
permissive, the hybrid refuses only when \emph{both} the vector top is below the floor
\emph{and} the graph links no seed; the BM25 channel is a recall booster, never a grounding
signal. \S8 reports the consequence.

\section{The corpus}
\texttt{corpus/generate.py} deterministically builds \textbf{Meridian Field Services}, a
fictional industrial field-services company, from a single seeded RNG. Population sizes are
fixed constants (seed-independent), so the corpus size is stable across machines; the seed
controls only the random wiring and phrasing. Every fact is derived from exactly one graph
edge or node attribute, so every fact has a known subject, relation, object, and set of
linked entities.

\begin{table}[h]\centering\small
\begin{tabular}{llp{7.6cm}}
\toprule
Layer & Count & Notes \\
\midrule
Facts & 1{,}583 & one NL statement per edge/attribute \\
Entities & 414 & 60 people, 45 equipment, 20 sites, 12 clients, 10 vendors, 6 teams, 6 certs, 150 jobs, 40 incidents, $+$ city/date nodes \\
Edges & 1{,}498 & directed, typed \\
Relations & 22 & \textbf{closed} vocabulary (\texttt{REPORTS\_TO}, \texttt{USES}, \texttt{MAINTAINED\_BY}, \dots) \\
Entity types & 11 & closed vocabulary \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Substrate inventory of the frozen corpus (seed 42).}
\end{table}

Fact composition mirrors a real operational store: \textbf{jobs dominate row count}
(834 facts, 53\%) the way email exhaust dominates the production store in~[2], while org,
equipment, incident, and site facts make up the rest. This is deliberate, it lets
known-item queries stratify across categories while the bulk sits in one operational
category, reproducing the ``row count is not retrieval demand'' property~[2] observed in
production. The generator writes \texttt{facts.jsonl}, \texttt{graph.json}, and a
\texttt{manifest.json} carrying SHA-256 checksums of both; the committed corpus is verified
against a fresh regeneration in the test suite.

\section{The query sets}
All three query sets are built by \texttt{harness/build\_queries.py} from the corpus and its
ground-truth graph, \textbf{before any arm is run}, and committed with a seed and checksums.
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Known-item (150).} A natural question whose answer is one specific fact,
  generated from functional relations across all five categories and phrased with paraphrase
  (``Who is X's supervisor?'' for a \texttt{REPORTS\_TO} fact). Ground truth: the gold fact
  id and its answer entity.
  \item \textbf{Multi-hop (30).} A question whose answer requires joining 2-3 facts
  (22 two-hop, 8 three-hop), from ten templates over forward chains (e.g.\ \emph{job
  $\rightarrow$ USES $\rightarrow$ equipment $\rightarrow$ MAINTAINED\_BY $\rightarrow$
  vendor}). Ground truth: the terminal fact that yields the answer entity, the full chain,
  and the answer entity. Every chain is verified forward-reachable from a query-mentioned
  seed.
  \item \textbf{Distractor (20).} A well-formed question about an entity that is
  \textbf{not} in the corpus. The correct behavior is refusal. The test suite asserts each
  distractor links to zero real entities, a clean absence probe.
\end{itemize}
The distractors are the sharpest instrument: phrased identically to answerable known-item
queries but naming an absent entity, they are lexically and semantically close to real
facts. That is what makes them a fabrication-pressure test: a dense retriever will find
\emph{something} similar and must decide whether to answer.

\section{Method}
Three instruments, all deterministic and seeded. (1)~\textbf{Retrieval replay}: each arm is
built once over the 1{,}583-fact corpus, then answers all 200 queries; known-item scored at
cutoffs \{1,5,20\} from a depth-20 recall, multi-hop at top-10, distractors on the
\texttt{refused} flag. (2)~\textbf{Latency microbenchmark}: every \texttt{recall} call is
wall-clock timed after a per-arm warmup; p50/p95 reported over all 200 queries and
host-dependent, as in~[2]. (3)~\textbf{Tracing}: every recall is emitted as an
OpenTelemetry-shaped span and exported to \texttt{traces/<arm>/} as OTLP JSON, shipping a
standard-tooling artifact and closing the ``own-telemetry-only'' gap~[2] flagged, as a side
effect. The headline numbers use \texttt{all-MiniLM-L6-v2} on CPU (deterministic to float32
given the pinned version); a dependency-free hashing embedder (\texttt{AMA\_EMBEDDER=hash})
lets the harness run fully offline, and because all three arms share whichever embedder is
selected, the architectural comparison is valid under either.

The first surprise is at the top of the table: \textbf{pure RAG is the weakest known-item
arm}, and by a wide margin at depth (Recall@20 of 75.3\% means one gold fact in four never
surfaces in the top 20). The cause is directional confusion in a duplicate-rich store: a
query like ``Who is Dana Reyes's supervisor?'' is embedded close to every ``\emph{X} reports
to Dana Reyes'' fact where Dana is the \emph{object}, and those crowd out the single fact
where she is the subject. The graph arm has no such problem, it links the subject and
returns that subject's small set of outgoing facts (Recall@20 $=100$\%). The hybrid wins
ranking precision (Recall@1 71.3\%, MRR 0.800). \textbf{H1 is refuted:} on an entity-anchored
corpus, structure beats pure semantics even for single-fact retrieval.

\section{Where the hybrid loses}
A hybrid that won everything would be a benchmark artifact. This one loses three ways, two of
them by inheriting the failure modes of the channels it fuses, and the honest accounting is
that its \emph{only} wins on this corpus are ranking-precision (Recall@1, MRR).

\paragraph{1. It re-inherits RAG's fabrication (the expensive loss).} The hybrid contains the
very channel, graph linking, whose absence signal drives the graph arm to a perfect 100\%
on distractors. It still fabricates at RAG's rate (10\%) because its grounding rule is a
permissive OR: it answers if \emph{either} the vector channel clears the floor \emph{or} the
graph links a seed. On a distractor the graph correctly abstains, but the vector channel
clears the floor and the OR lets it speak. This is the general hazard of naive multi-channel
grounding: \textbf{union-of-evidence maximizes recall and minimizes refusal}, and refusal is
where fabrication lives. A graph-\emph{veto} rule would recover the 100\%, at the cost of
over-refusing vector-answerable queries the graph cannot link. That trade is real and
unmeasured here; it is the single highest-leverage follow-up (\S12).

\paragraph{2. Fusion dilutes the graph channel on multi-hop (the subtle loss).} The hybrid
trails graph-only on every multi-hop measure (40.0\% vs.\ 53.3\% terminal success) \emph{even
with the graph channel up-weighted 2:1}. Reciprocal Rank Fusion rewards cross-channel
agreement, but a multi-hop terminal fact is found by exactly one channel, the graph, while
the two high-recall channels agree on shallower, surface-similar facts and outvote it.
Weighting the graph channel higher narrows the gap (unweighted RRF scored 13.3\%; the 2:1
weight lifts it to 40.0\%) but does not close it. Fusion, the mechanism that buys the hybrid
its known-item ranking win, is the same mechanism that costs it multi-hop recall.

\paragraph{3. It is the most expensive arm on every cost axis (the unavoidable loss).} Three
materialized indices (vectors $+$ BM25 $+$ graph) make it the largest store at 2{,}528
bytes/fact, and three channels plus a query embedding make it the slowest at $\approx$9\,ms
p50. It is strictly Pareto-dominated by the graph arm on latency, storage, multi-hop, and
refusal simultaneously; it beats the graph arm only on MRR (0.800 vs.\ 0.750) and Recall@1
(71.3\% vs.\ 62.0\%). The honest one-line valuation: \textbf{it buys ${\sim}9$ points of
Recall@1 and 0.05 of MRR for ${\sim}30\times$ the latency, $25\times$ the storage, and a
90-point refusal regression.} What rescues the hybrid is not visible in this corpus at all
(\S10), and naming that is the price of credibility.

\section{Failure inventory}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{RAG, directional confusion (known-item).} 24.7\% of gold facts never reach
  RAG's top-20; the dominant mode is subject/object inversion in symmetric-looking relations.
  \item \textbf{RAG, no-join (multi-hop).} 30/30 terminal misses (5.0\% chain coverage); no
  mechanism to follow an edge.
  \item \textbf{RAG \& hybrid, confident fabrication (distractor).} 18/20 distractors
  answered; the nearest real fact clears the 0.40 floor and neither arm has a structural
  absence signal to override it.
  \item \textbf{Graph, terminal-rank burial (multi-hop).} 14/30 terminal misses despite 81\%
  chain coverage; hop-ascending order front-loads the anchor's shallow facts and Jaccard
  cannot distinguish the paraphrased terminal. All 8 three-hop queries fail this way.
  \item \textbf{Graph, paraphrase ranking (known-item MRR).} MRR 0.750 trails the hybrid's
  0.800 when the query shares no salient token with the gold fact.
  \item \textbf{Hybrid, fusion dilution.} See \S8.2.
\end{itemize}

\section{Threats to validity}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{The corpus is the graph's best case, and we say so first.} Meridian is
  synthetic, fully canonical (one clean id per entity, a closed relation vocabulary, zero
  extraction noise), and its queries are entity-anchored. That is precisely the regime in
  which entity linking and traversal dominate and dense semantics are least needed. Real
  operational memory, the production setting~[2] the hybrid was built for, is the opposite:
  noisy distilled text, no clean ids in the user's phrasing, heavy paraphrase and
  coreference. The single most likely way these results \emph{fail to generalize} is that
  they understate the vector and hybrid arms in exactly the conditions those arms exist to
  handle.
  \item \textbf{Known-item queries name the anchor entity,} handing the graph and hybrid arms
  a free, reliable seed, the primary driver of their known-item lead.
  \item \textbf{Multi-hop success is a strict top-10 terminal-fact bar,} undercounting the
  graph arm's 81\% chain coverage.
  \item \textbf{Single embedder, single host.} A stronger embedder would lift the vector arms
  uniformly; latency is from one 2-vCPU-class host.
  \item \textbf{The refusal thresholds are pre-committed, not optimal.} RAG's 0.40 floor is
  inherited from~[2], not tuned on this corpus; we fixed it in advance to avoid post-hoc
  curve-fitting.
  \item \textbf{The hybrid is one point in a design space.} Weighted RRF with a 2:1 graph
  weight and OR-grounding is a reasonable, minimal hybrid, not the optimal one; \S8 names two
  variants that would move its numbers.
  \item \textbf{No LLM in the loop.} These are retrieval metrics, not end-task answer-accuracy
  metrics.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Discussion}
\textbf{The hypotheses, revisited.} H2 and H4 are confirmed. H1 and H3 are \textbf{refuted}:
pure RAG was the \emph{worst} known-item arm on an entity-anchored corpus, and the hybrid did
not sit between graph and RAG on refusal, it was pinned to RAG's fabrication rate. Refuting
one's own hypotheses is the intended outcome of a real ablation.

\textbf{The substrate thesis, ablated.} [2] argued that recall quality is governed by store
structure, not model choice. This study sharpens it: on a clean corpus, \emph{structure
alone} (the graph arm, no model on the hot path) is competitive-to-dominant, and the dense
channel's marginal contribution is ranking precision plus the coverage the graph lacks when
queries are not entity-anchored, which this corpus, by construction, does not test. The
production hybrid's justification therefore lives in the gap between this corpus and real
operational text, and this study locates that gap rather than papering over it.

\textbf{What the fabrication result means for hybrids generally.} The most transferable
finding is architectural: \textbf{fusing a high-precision abstaining channel with a
high-recall answering channel under union-of-evidence grounding throws away the abstention.}
The graph arm's zero-fabrication property is a design asset a naive hybrid destroys. Any
hybrid that wants both recall and calibrated refusal must make refusal a \emph{veto} of the
grounded channel, not a \emph{vote}.

\section{Recommended next steps}
In order of leverage: (1)~\textbf{graph-veto refusal} in the hybrid, measuring recovered
distractor refusal against induced over-refusal; (2)~\textbf{rank-aware fusion} that
preserves a lone deep-hop graph hit; (3)~a \textbf{paraphrase / descriptive-reference query
set} that does not name the anchor entity, the single most important generalization test;
(4)~a \textbf{noisy corpus variant} (extraction noise, alias drift, coreference) moving
Meridian toward production conditions; (5)~an \textbf{LLM answer-accuracy layer} that converts
retrieval metrics into task metrics.

\section{Conclusion}
Three memory architectures, one frozen 1{,}583-fact corpus, 200 seeded queries, one eval
harness: on this clean, canonical corpus the \textbf{graph-only arm wins or ties most
axes}, Recall@20 100\%, multi-hop 53.3\%, refusal 100\%, at $\approx$0.3\,ms and 101
bytes/fact, while the \textbf{hybrid's measured advantage reduces to ranking precision}
(Recall@1 71.3\%, MRR 0.800), bought at ${\sim}30\times$ the latency, $25\times$ the storage,
and a 90-point refusal regression it inherits wholesale from its dense channel. Two of four
pre-registered hypotheses are refuted. The corpus is disclosed as the graph's most favorable
case, which is exactly where the honest boundary of the claim lies: this study does not show
that hybrids are unnecessary; it shows that on canonical, entity-anchored memory the
specialized graph is hard to beat and cheap to run, that a naive hybrid throws away the
graph's zero-fabrication property, and that the production hybrid's real justification lives
in the messier corpus this study deliberately did not build, and names as the next one to.

\paragraph{Practitioner one-liner.} On a clean, canonical operational corpus, a vector-free
knowledge graph beats plain RAG and ties or beats a fused hybrid on almost everything that
matters, recall, multi-hop, and especially refusal (100\% vs.\ 10\%), at ${\sim}1/25$th the
storage and ${\sim}1/30$th the latency; the hybrid's extra machinery buys ranking precision
and, unless refusal is a veto rather than a vote, silently reinherits RAG's fabrication.

\paragraph{Reproducibility.} A stranger reproduces every number with
\texttt{pip install -r requirements.txt} then \texttt{make eval} (or
\texttt{python -m harness.run\_eval}), which regenerates the frozen corpus and query sets
(verifying committed checksums), builds all three arms, runs the 200 queries, and writes
\texttt{results/*.csv}, \texttt{results/tables.md} (the source of Tables 1-4),
\texttt{results/summary.json}, \texttt{traces/<arm>/} (OTLP spans), and the figures. All
retrieval metrics are deterministic given the pinned \texttt{all-MiniLM-L6-v2}; only latency
(Table 4) is host-dependent. \texttt{AMA\_EMBEDDER=hash} runs the whole study offline with no
model download. A 25-test suite (\texttt{make test}) fails if the committed artifacts drift
from their seeds.
